What Are Rights
& Responsibilities?

⚖️
What are RIGHTS?
Zimbabwe Constitution 2013, Chapter 4

Rights are entitlements and freedoms that every person is born with or is guaranteed by law. They protect individuals from abuse, discrimination, and unfair treatment.

In Zimbabwe, rights are enshrined in the Constitution of Zimbabwe (2013), Chapter 4 — the Declaration of Rights. This chapter protects every teenager regardless of gender, race, or background.

🌟
What are RESPONSIBILITIES?
Section 44 — Obligation to respect rights

Responsibilities are duties and obligations that individuals owe to themselves, their families, and to society. Section 44 of Zimbabwe's Constitution states that every person must respect and uphold the rights of others.

Your responsibilities exist at school, in your community, and at home.

Rights vs Responsibilities
⚖️ RIGHTS🌟 RESPONSIBILITIES
Something you are entitled toSomething you are expected to do
Protected by ConstitutionRequired by law & society
You can CLAIM themYou must FULFIL them
e.g. Right to Educatione.g. Attending school regularly
🔄
Remember: Rights and responsibilities are two sides of the same coin. No right is absolute — balancing it with your responsibility to others is what makes Zimbabwe a constitutional democracy.

📝 STUDY MATERIALS

Study Notes

Constitutional Basics
  • Rights are in Zimbabwe's Constitution (2013), Chapter 4.
  • Section 48: Right to life — the State must protect it.
  • Section 49: Right to personal liberty — no unlawful detention.
  • Section 50: Rights of arrested persons — call parents & lawyer within 48hrs.
  • Section 51: Right to human dignity — inherent in every person.
  • Section 52: Personal security — freedom from all forms of violence.
  • Section 53: Freedom from torture — ABSOLUTE, cannot be limited.
  • Section 54: Freedom from forced labour and slavery.
  • Section 56: Equality and non-discrimination on ALL grounds.
  • Section 57: Right to privacy — home, property, communications.
Freedoms
  • Section 58: Freedom of assembly and association — join clubs, groups.
  • Section 59: Freedom to demonstrate and petition.
  • Section 60: Freedom of conscience, thought, religion, and belief.
  • Section 61: Freedom of expression — speak, write, create art.
  • Section 62: Freedom of the media.
  • Section 63: Access to information, language, and culture.
  • Section 64: Freedom of profession, trade, or occupation.
  • Section 65: Labour rights — fair wages, safe conditions.
  • Section 66: Freedom of movement and residence.
  • Section 67: Political rights — vote at 18, SRC before that.
Social & Economic Rights
  • Section 72: Right to agricultural land — food security.
  • Section 73: Environmental rights — clean, healthy environment.
  • Section 75: Right to education — free, state-funded basic education.
  • Section 76: Right to health care — emergency care cannot be refused.
  • Section 77: Right to food and water — safe, clean, and sufficient.
  • Section 78: Marriage rights — no forced or child marriage.
  • Section 80: Rights of women — equal rights, dignity.
  • Section 81: Rights of school children — protection, nutrition, shelter.
  • Rights can only be limited by law under Section 86 — if reasonable and justifiable.
School Responsibilities
  • Attending school is both a right (S.75) and a responsibility.
  • Respecting teachers upholds the school community's dignity (S.51).
  • Bullying violates victims' rights under S.51 (dignity) and S.52 (security).
  • Completing work honours the education the State provides.
  • Joining SRC gives you democratic practice before you turn 18.
  • Reporting problems prevents rights violations from escalating.
  • Section 44: All persons must respect and uphold rights of others.
Community & Home
  • Section 73 gives you the right to a clean environment — your duty is to keep Eastview clean.
  • Saying NO to crime protects everyone's rights and your own future.
  • Respecting parents/guardians shows care for those responsible for your welfare.
  • Helping at home builds life skills and strengthens family bonds.
  • Participating in community meetings exercises your democratic rights.
  • Ubuntu — "I am because we are" — is at the heart of Zimbabwean community life.
